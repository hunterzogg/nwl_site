Methodology: Every QB/RB/WR/TE pick is graded on value (did they outperform where we drafted them) and production (was the season just good, full stop), weighted equally and curved into a percentile-based grade.
Show full methodology

Position draft rank is where a player went in our draft (the 3rd WR taken is WR3, the 12th WR taken is WR12, regardless of round or overall pick), compared against position finish rank, where they actually finished among all NFL players at that position that season by half-PPR points — that gap is the value signal. Production is simply how good the season was, full stop, regardless of draft slot — a top-3 finish at the position counts as strong production even if it came a couple of spots below where they were drafted. Blending both matters: WR3 finishing as WR18 is a bad miss on value alone, WR25 finishing as WR15 is a great value pick, and an RB1 finishing as RB3 grades well even though it's technically a -2 slip in rank, because a top-3 running back season is exactly what an RB1 pick is for. Grades are curved on a percentile basis across all 154 manager-seasons (for a single draft) or all 13 managers (for the overall ranking) — so grades are always relative to how everyone else in the league drafted, not an absolute bar. Value Score (0–100) is that percentile, so a Value Score of 80 means that draft (or manager) out-valued about 80% of the league.

Half-PPR stats sourced from nflverse's public play-by-play data. About 1.4% of graded picks (mostly season-ending injuries, suspensions, or holdouts with zero recorded stats that season) are scored as a full bust — that's the correct outcome for a pick that produced nothing, not a data gap.
